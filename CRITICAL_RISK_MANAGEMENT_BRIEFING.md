## 1. Executive Summary

Critical risk management is a discipline that goes beyond conventional risk registers and compliance checklists. It is the structured, evidence-based practice of identifying the small number of risks in an organisation that can cause catastrophic, irreversible harm — and then rigorously verifying that the controls preventing those outcomes are actually working.

The evidence from major industrial disasters — from Texas City (2005) to Pike River (2010) to Dreamworld (2016) — consistently shows one pattern: organisations often possessed documented controls, completed checklists, and apparently functional management systems, yet critical controls had degraded, were bypassed, or had never worked as intended. The systems *said* the controls were in place. Reality said otherwise.

This briefing provides:

- A rigorous, source-grounded explanation of what critical risk management and critical controls are and how they work.
- A practical framework for designing, implementing, and sustaining a Critical Risk and Critical Control Management System (CRCCMS).
- Manager education content, including key teaching points, common misconceptions, and field-ready conversation guides.
- A full suite of document, template, and tool recommendations.
- Maturity assessment criteria.
- A 90-day implementation plan and capability development pathway.

**The core message for executive leadership:** Critical risk management is not a safety department initiative — it is an organisational survival function. The question is not whether your organisation has controls. It is whether those controls are effective, owned, verified, and improving.

## 2. Expert Overview of Critical Risk and Critical Controls

### 2.1 The Problem Critical Risk Management Solves

Most organisations manage risk through a combination of:
- Risk registers that score likelihood and consequence,
- Management systems that specify what controls *should* exist,
- Audits and inspections that check whether procedures are documented,
- Incident reporting systems that capture what went wrong after the fact.

The fundamental flaw in this model is that it conflates **having a control** with **having an effective control**. A procedure that has never been read, a safety device that has been bypassed, a training record that was auto-signed — these all appear as "controls in place" on paper but provide no actual protection.

Critical risk management addresses this by:

1. Identifying the small number of hazardous scenarios that could produce catastrophic outcomes.
2. Determining which specific controls are essential to prevent or mitigate those outcomes.
3. Establishing clear performance standards for those controls — what "working" means.
4. Verifying, continuously and systematically, whether controls are performing to standard.
5. Escalating and acting when they are not.

This approach is grounded in barrier theory, bowtie methodology, and the practical lessons from process safety and major hazard management.

### 2.2 Why This Matters in High-Risk Operations

In high-risk operational environments — including mining, oil and gas, construction, heavy manufacturing, chemical processing, utilities, transport, and attractions/amusement — the consequence of control failure is not a minor injury or a near-miss that prompts a procedure update. It is death, mass casualties, catastrophic asset damage, or irreversible environmental harm.

In these environments:
- The frequency of catastrophic events is typically low, so warning signals can be missed or dismissed.
- Workers adapt to imperfect conditions ("normalisation of deviance"), reducing perceived risk.
- System complexity means no individual understands the full exposure.
- Time and production pressure systematically erode control integrity.

Critical risk management counters these dynamics by installing a permanent verification engine that operates regardless of whether an incident has recently occurred.

### 2.3 The Regulatory and Legal Context

In Australia, the Work Health and Safety Act 2011 (Cwlth model law, adopted in most jurisdictions including Queensland) imposes a duty on persons conducting a business or undertaking (PCBUs) to eliminate risks to health and safety so far as is reasonably practicable, and where elimination is not reasonably practicable, to minimise those risks so far as is reasonably practicable (WHS Act 2011, s.17-18).

The *reasonably practicable* standard is defined in section 18 and requires consideration of the likelihood of the hazard occurring, the degree of harm, the availability and suitability of ways to eliminate or minimise the risk, and the cost. Importantly, high consequence must be given significant weight even where frequency is low — exactly the domain of critical risk management.

Queensland's *Safety in Recreational Water Activities Act 2011* and various industry-specific regulations (including for amusement rides under the *Plant Design Registration* requirements and the *Work Health and Safety Regulation 2011*) impose additional duties around registered plant, operator competency, and maintenance.

In major hazard facility (MHF) contexts under Queensland's *Work Health and Safety Regulation 2011* (Division 3, Chapter 9), there are explicit requirements for safety management systems, hazard identification, control measures, and periodic safety assessment — requirements that directly align with critical control management principles.

Safe Work Australia's *Model Code of Practice: How to Manage Work Health and Safety Risks* (2021) provides the foundational framework of hierarchy of controls and risk management process.

### 2.4 The Intellectual Heritage of Critical Control Management

Critical control management draws from several intellectual traditions:

**Process Safety / Barrier Management:** Originating in the chemical process industries, this tradition — articulated by the Center for Chemical Process Safety (CCPS) and reflected in standards such as IEC 61511 (Functional Safety: Safety Instrumented Systems) — establishes the concept of independent layers of protection. A single layer is never relied upon; multiple independent barriers are required, and each must be verified.

**Bowtie Analysis:** Developed in the offshore oil and gas industry in the 1990s and systematised by organisations including Shell, DNV, and CGE Risk, bowtie analysis provides a visual representation of hazard scenarios as a "bow tie" — threats on the left driving toward a top event, and consequences on the right — with barriers/controls sitting on the bow ties' "ties."

**Resilience Engineering and Safety-II:** Work by Erik Hollnagel, David Woods, and Sidney Dekker has shifted safety thinking from purely failure-focused models to understanding what makes systems succeed under variable conditions. Critical control management must account for human performance variability, adaptive capacity, and the reality that procedures are never perfectly followed.

**Human and Organisational Factors:** Research by Reason (1990, "Human Error"), Rasmussen (skills-rules-knowledge framework), and Turner ("Man-Made Disasters," 1978) demonstrates that most catastrophic events have long incubation periods where control degradation accumulates silently. A robust critical control system must surface these early warning signals.

**ICMM Critical Control Management (2015):** The International Council on Mining and Metals published its *Critical Control Management: Implementation Guide* in 2015, which has become the most widely referenced practical framework for critical control management in high-hazard industries. It is explicitly implementation-focused and is the primary reference for many Australian mining and resources operations.

## 3. Definitions and Key Concepts

### 3.1 Hazard

A **hazard** is a situation or condition with the potential to cause harm. Hazards are the starting point for all risk management work.

*Example:* A 5-tonne counterweight suspended above a maintenance access zone.

*Source:* Safe Work Australia, *Model Code of Practice: How to Manage Work Health and Safety Risks* (2021); ISO 45001:2018, clause 3.19.

### 3.2 Risk

**Risk** is the effect of uncertainty on objectives. In safety contexts, it is conventionally understood as the combination of the likelihood of a harmful event and its consequence.

*Source:* ISO 31000:2018, clause 3.1; ISO 45001:2018, clause 3.21.

**Note on risk scoring:** Conventional 5×5 likelihood/consequence matrices provide a rough ranking tool but have significant limitations — they do not verify that controls are working, and they can mask high-consequence/low-frequency scenarios behind acceptable scores. Critical risk management supplements or partially replaces matrix-based scoring with control-effectiveness-based assessment.

### 3.3 Unwanted Event / Material Unwanted Event (MUE)

An **unwanted event** is the central event in a bowtie — the moment when control of a hazard is lost and a consequence chain is initiated. In critical risk language, this is often called a **material unwanted event (MUE)** or **top event** — the event that must be prevented or limited.

*Example:* Loss of containment of a suspended load (leading to dropped-object consequences). Loss of control of a ride vehicle. Uncontrolled energy release during electrical maintenance.

### 3.4 Consequence

The **consequence** is the outcome — the harm that results from an unwanted event. In critical risk management, we focus on the **worst credible consequence** — the most severe outcome that could realistically occur if controls fail, rather than the most likely minor outcome.

*Example:* Fatality or multiple fatalities; catastrophic structural failure; mass-casualty event at a public attraction.

### 3.5 Critical Risk

A **critical risk** is a risk that, if not controlled, has the potential to result in a fatality, multiple fatalities, or catastrophic harm to people, assets, environment, or community.

**Key distinction from general risk:** Not every risk is critical. Critical risks are identified because:
- Their worst credible consequence is catastrophic (typically defined as death, multiple deaths, or irreversible mass harm), AND
- They require specific, dedicated controls to prevent that outcome, AND
- Those controls need to be proactively verified rather than assumed to be working.

*Source:* ICMM, *Critical Control Management: Implementation Guide* (2015), section 2.1.

In the ICMM model, a critical risk is typically called a **material unwanted event (MUE)** — the event that must not be allowed to occur.

**Typical critical risk categories in high-hazard industries:**
- Working at height / falls from elevation
- Dropped objects
- Ground/structure failure (mining, construction)
- Electrical energy contact
- Mechanical energy / unplanned energy release
- Confined space entry
- Fire and explosion
- Mobile plant / vehicle interaction
- Chemical exposure / toxic release
- Structural collapse or ride/attraction mechanical failure

### 3.6 Control

A **control** is any measure that eliminates or modifies a risk. Controls exist on two sides of the unwanted event:

- **Preventive controls** (left side of bowtie): Prevent the hazard from escalating to the unwanted event. They break the threat pathway.
- **Mitigating controls** (right side of bowtie): Reduce the consequences after the unwanted event has occurred. They limit harm once loss of control is initiated.

*Source:* ICMM (2015), section 2.2; Safe Work Australia hierarchy of controls.

### 3.7 Critical Control

A **critical control** is a control that, if absent or failed, would significantly increase the risk of a critical outcome occurring, despite the existence of other controls.

A critical control has a **decisive** relationship with the unwanted event — its removal or failure alone can result in catastrophic harm.

**Distinguishing criteria for critical controls:**

1. **Consequence test:** If this control is absent or fails, does the potential consequence escalate to catastrophic? If yes, it is critical.
2. **Specificity:** Critical controls are specific, definable actions, devices, systems, or behaviours — not general management activities.
3. **Verifiability:** Critical controls can be observed, tested, or measured. Performance standards can be written for them.
4. **Independence:** Ideally, critical controls are independent of other controls — their failure does not automatically cause other controls to fail.

*Source:* ICMM (2015), section 2.3; CCPS, *Layer of Protection Analysis* (2001).

**Example critical controls (Working at Height MUE):**
- Edge protection / guardrails that meet specification (preventive)
- Approved and inspected fall arrest harness and anchor (preventive)
- Rescue capability within X minutes (mitigating)
- Emergency response plan tested to standard (mitigating)

**Note on what is NOT a critical control:**
- A safety induction (too general; does not directly prevent the specific event)
- A risk assessment register entry
- A management system procedure
- A generic safety culture initiative

### 3.8 General Controls vs Critical Controls

| Dimension | General Controls | Critical Controls |
|-----------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Scope | Broad; apply across many hazards | Specific; address one critical scenario |
| Consequence if missing | May increase minor/moderate risk | Absence alone can cause catastrophic outcome |
| Verification requirement | Standard audit/inspection | Dedicated, frequent, structured verification |
| Ownership | Team/department | Named individual (control owner) |
| Performance standard | Often implicit/procedural | Explicit, measurable, documented standard |
| Reporting frequency | Periodic | Continuous / real-time status |

### 3.9 Barrier

A **barrier** is a means — physical, human, or administrative — that prevents an unwanted event from occurring or escalating. In barrier theory, each barrier is a layer of protection.

A critical control is typically a specific, verified barrier. Not all barriers qualify as critical controls — only those that are decisive for preventing or mitigating the critical outcome.

*Source:* Hollnagel, *Barriers and Accident Prevention* (2004); CCPS, *Layer of Protection Analysis* (2001).

### 3.10 Control Performance Standard

A **control performance standard (CPS)** is a documented specification of how a critical control must perform to be considered effective. It specifies:

- **What** the control must do (its function)
- **How well** it must do it (performance specification)
- **How it is validated and verified**
- **Who is responsible** (owner)
- **What triggers action** if it fails

CPS are the operational backbone of critical control management. Without them, verification is subjective.

*Source:* UK Health and Safety Executive, *Major Hazard Aspects of the Transport of Dangerous Substances* (HSG 260); ICMM (2015), section 3.

### 3.11 Verification vs Validation

- **Validation** confirms that the control was designed and installed correctly — it will do what it was designed to do (typically a one-time or periodic technical check).
- **Verification** confirms that the control is *currently* performing to its standard — it is doing its job right now.

Critical control management is primarily concerned with **ongoing verification** — the real-time or periodic confirmation that controls are in place and effective.

### 3.12 Assurance

**Assurance** is the overall confidence that critical controls are effective. It is built through multiple, independent verification activities:

- **First-party assurance (Line 1):** The person doing the task / the control owner verifies the control themselves (pre-task checks, equipment inspections).
- **Second-party assurance (Line 2):** Supervisors, managers, or HSE professionals observe and verify control performance (field leadership interactions, management field visits).
- **Third-party assurance (Line 3):** Independent internal or external audits, reviews, or inspections.

*Source:* Three Lines of Defence model (Institute of Internal Auditors); ISO 19011:2018 (Guidelines for Auditing Management Systems).

### 3.13 ALARP / SFAIRP

**ALARP** (As Low As Reasonably Practicable) and **SFAIRP** (So Far As Is Reasonably Practicable) are the UK and Australian legal standards respectively for risk reduction. Both require that risk reduction measures be implemented unless the cost or difficulty is grossly disproportionate to the benefit gained.

In practice, for critical risks, this means:
- All available critical controls must be in place unless a specific case for gross disproportion can be made.
- The standard is not "too expensive" — it is "grossly disproportionate."
- Regulators and courts will scrutinise decisions not to implement controls that would have prevented a fatality.

*Source:* WHS Act 2011, s.18; Safe Work Australia, *Interpretive Guideline: Reasonably Practicable* (2011); UK HSE, *Reducing Risks, Protecting People (R2P2)* (2001).

### 3.14 Leading and Lagging Indicators

- **Lagging indicators** measure outcomes that have already occurred: injury rates, incidents, fatalities. They confirm that something went wrong but provide no advance warning.
- **Leading indicators** measure the presence and effectiveness of controls *before* failure: verification completion rates, control health scores, overdue actions, near-miss rates, field observation findings.

Critical control management is fundamentally a **leading indicator** system — it measures control effectiveness rather than waiting for harm to occur.

*Source:* Safe Work Australia, *Leading and Lagging Indicators* (2013); Hopkins, *Failure to Learn: The BP Texas City Refinery Disaster* (2008).

### 3.15 Normalisation of Deviance

**Normalisation of deviance** (Vaughan, 1996) describes the process by which deviations from standard practice become accepted over time because they have not yet caused harm. Workers and managers gradually accept degraded control states as normal.

This is one of the primary mechanisms of critical control failure — and a key reason why critical controls must be actively verified rather than assumed to be maintained.

*Source:* Vaughan, D., *The Challenger Launch Decision* (1996); Dekker, S., *The Field Guide to Understanding Human Error* (2006).

## 4. Comparison of Major Frameworks and Source Models

### 4.1 ICMM Critical Control Management Framework (2015)

**Source:** International Council on Mining and Metals, *Critical Control Management: Implementation Guide* (2015).

**Overview:** The most widely adopted practical framework for critical control management in high-hazard industries. Developed specifically to address the gap between documented controls and actual control performance.

**Core components:**
1. Identification of material unwanted events (MUEs) — the small number of catastrophic scenarios the organisation must prevent.
2. Development of bowties for each MUE — visual mapping of threats, unwanted event, consequences, and controls.
3. Identification of critical controls from the bowtie — the specific controls whose failure would alone cause catastrophic harm.
4. Development of critical control performance standards — measurable specifications for each critical control.
5. Assignment of critical control ownership — named accountable individuals.
6. Verification routines — scheduled checks confirming control performance.
7. Trigger action response plans (TARPs) — defined response actions when controls fail or degrade.
8. Management reporting and review.

**Strengths:** Practical, field-tested, implementation-focused, widely referenced by regulators and industry in Australia and internationally.

**Limitations:** Mining-sector oriented; requires adaptation for other industries. Does not fully address human factors or complex sociotechnical systems.

### 4.2 Bowtie Methodology

**Source:** CGE Risk Management / BowTie Pro; Shell's original "bow tie" development; referenced in UK HSE guidance; Energy Institute *Guidance on the Use of Bowtie Diagrams* (2013).

**Overview:** Bowtie analysis is a risk visualisation and analysis tool that maps the relationship between:
- **Threats** (causes that could initiate the unwanted event) — left side
- **Top event** (the unwanted event itself) — centre
- **Consequences** (outcomes if the top event is not controlled) — right side
- **Preventive barriers/controls** — on the left ties, between threats and top event
- **Mitigating barriers/controls** — on the right ties, between top event and consequences
- **Escalation factors** — conditions that undermine barriers
- **Escalation factor controls** — controls on the escalation factors themselves

**Key uses:**
- Visual communication of risk to management and workers
- Identification of critical controls
- Basis for developing performance standards
- Assurance mapping
- Incident investigation (what barriers failed?)

**Strengths:** Highly visual; communicates complex risk scenarios accessibly; directly links threats, controls, and consequences.

**Limitations:** Can become overly complex if not disciplined; requires facilitated development to be rigorous; quality depends heavily on facilitator and team knowledge. Software tools (BowTiePro, BowTie XP, ARM BowtieXP, Safety Barrier Manager) assist but are not substitutes for good analysis.

*Source:* Energy Institute, *Guidance on the Use of Bowtie Diagrams in the Management of Major Accident Hazards* (2013).

### 4.3 Barrier-Based Risk Management (Swiss Cheese Model)

**Source:** Reason, J., *Human Error* (1990); Reason, J., *Managing the Risks of Organisational Accidents* (1997).

**Overview:** James Reason's "Swiss cheese model" conceptualises each layer of protection as a slice of cheese with holes representing failures or gaps. An accident occurs when the holes in multiple slices align — when every barrier fails simultaneously.

**Key implications:**
- No single barrier should be relied upon.
- Multiple independent barriers increase resilience.
- Organisations must understand the "holes" in their barriers and work to reduce them.
- Latent failures (organisational weaknesses) create conditions for active failures (human errors).

**Strengths:** Intuitive; excellent for management communication; highlights systemic rather than individual-blame perspectives.

**Limitations:** Simplified model; does not fully capture dynamic, complex systems; has been critiqued by resilience engineering scholars for being overly failure-focused.

### 4.4 Layer of Protection Analysis (LOPA)

**Source:** CCPS, *Layer of Protection Analysis: Simplified Process Risk Assessment* (2001); IEC 61511.

**Overview:** LOPA is a semi-quantitative risk assessment method used primarily in process safety. It evaluates whether the independent protection layers (IPLs) for a given scenario are sufficient to reduce risk to a tolerable level.

**Key concepts:**
- Each independent protection layer must be genuinely independent — failure of one cannot cause failure of another.
- Each layer has an associated probability of failure on demand (PFD).
- Combined PFDs must reduce risk to within the organisation's tolerable risk criteria.

**Relevance to critical control management:** LOPA provides the analytical rigour underlying the concept of critical controls — only truly independent, reliable layers count. This is the origin of the principle that a critical control must be verifiable and must function independently.

*Source:* CCPS (2001); IEC 61511:2016 (Functional Safety: Safety Instrumented Systems for the Process Industry Sector).

### 4.5 ISO 31000:2018 Risk Management

**Source:** ISO 31000:2018, *Risk Management — Guidelines.*

**Overview:** The international standard for risk management principles and guidelines. Provides a framework and process applicable across all risk types.

**Relevant to critical control management:**
- Clause 6.3 (Risk identification): All risks must be identified, including catastrophic ones.
- Clause 6.4 (Risk analysis): Analysis must consider cause and effect chains.
- Clause 6.5 (Risk evaluation): Evaluation must determine whether risk is acceptable after controls.
- Clause 6.6 (Risk treatment): Treatment options include modifying likelihood or consequence through controls.
- Clause 6.7 (Monitoring and review): Continuous monitoring of controls is required.

**Note:** ISO 31000 provides the governance framework but not the operational methodology for critical control management. The ICMM, CCPS, and bowtie approaches provide operational methodology.

### 4.6 ISO 45001:2018 Occupational Health and Safety Management Systems

**Source:** ISO 45001:2018.

**Overview:** The international standard for OHS management systems. Replaced AS/NZS 4801 and OHSAS 18001 as the reference standard in Australia.

**Critical control management integration points:**
- Clause 6.1.2: Hazard identification and risk assessment — must include consideration of past incidents, foreseeable changes, and all significant hazards.
- Clause 6.1.3: Assessment of legal compliance and other requirements.
- Clause 8.1: Operational planning and control — systematic controls for identified risks.
- Clause 9.1: Performance evaluation — monitoring, measurement, and analysis.
- Clause 9.1.2: Legal compliance evaluation.
- Clause 9.2: Internal audit.
- Clause 10: Improvement and corrective action.

**Relationship to critical control management:** ISO 45001 provides the management system shell. Critical control management provides the specific operational substance — particularly around hazard identification, control hierarchy, verification, and performance measurement.

### 4.7 UK HSE Major Hazard Guidance — Barrier Management

**Source:** UK Health and Safety Executive, *Managing for Health and Safety (HSG65)* (3rd ed., 2013); *Major Hazards Aspects of the Transport of Dangerous Substances* (2006); Process Safety Leadership Group reports.

**Overview:** The UK HSE has developed extensive guidance on major hazard management, barrier management, and safety cases. Key concepts include:
- **Safety cases** for major hazard facilities — demonstrating that major accident hazards are adequately controlled.
- **Safety management systems** — the organisational structures and processes maintaining control.
- **Performance standards** for safety-critical elements — precisely equivalent to critical control performance standards.
- **Competent authority interface** — the relationship between operators and the regulator.

**Key HSE publications relevant to critical control management:**
- *Reducing Risks, Protecting People (R2P2)* — HSE's approach to ALARP.
- *HSG254: Developing Process Safety Indicators* — guidance on leading and lagging process safety indicators.
- *HSG65: Managing for Health and Safety* — the POPIMAR model (Policy, Organising, Planning, Implementing, Measuring, Audit, Review).

### 4.8 CCPS Process Safety Management

**Source:** American Institute of Chemical Engineers (AIChE) Center for Chemical Process Safety (CCPS), multiple publications including *Guidelines for Risk Based Process Safety* (2007).

**Overview:** CCPS has developed Risk Based Process Safety (RBPS) as a comprehensive process safety management framework. It identifies 20 elements including process safety culture, hazard identification, risk analysis, management of change, and process safety competency.

**Most relevant CCPS concepts:**
- **Bow ties and LOPA** — analytical tools for identifying and sizing barriers.
- **Management of change (MOC)** — formal process to assess and control changes that could affect critical controls.
- **Mechanical integrity** — systematic verification that safety-critical equipment is fit for purpose.
- **Process safety metrics** — API RP 754 provides the Tier 1-4 indicator framework (process safety events of varying severity).

*Source:* CCPS, *Guidelines for Risk Based Process Safety* (2007); API RP 754:2016, *Process Safety Performance Indicators for the Refining and Petrochemical Industries.*

### 4.9 Human and Organisational Factors (HOF)

**Source:** Reason (1990, 1997); Hollnagel (2004, 2014); Dekker (2006, 2012); UK HSE Human Factors guidance.

**Overview:** Human factors research demonstrates that:
- Human performance is variable, predictable, and influenced by system conditions (workload, design, training, procedures, supervision, culture).
- Most human errors in complex systems are system-induced rather than individual failures.
- Controls designed without attention to human factors will not perform reliably.

**Critical control management implications:**
- Critical controls involving human action must be designed for reliable human performance (not just written procedures).
- Verification of human-action controls must assess the conditions enabling reliable performance, not just whether the procedure was followed.
- Control degradation is often driven by organisational pressures (production, cost, schedule) that are not visible in standard safety reporting.

*Source:* Reason, J., *Managing the Risks of Organisational Accidents* (1997); UK HSE, *Human Factors: Inspectors Toolkit* (2002); Hopkins, A., *Failure to Learn* (2008).

## 5. Best-Practice System Design

### 5.1 Governance Architecture

A mature Critical Risk and Critical Control Management System (CRCCMS) has a clear governance architecture with four layers:

**Layer 1 — Strategic Governance (Board/Executive)**
- Approves the list of critical risks.
- Receives executive reporting on critical control health.
- Sets risk appetite and tolerance thresholds.
- Accountable for adequacy of resources for critical control management.

**Layer 2 — Operational Governance (Senior Leaders / BU Heads)**
- Oversees implementation within their area of responsibility.
- Reviews critical control performance data monthly.
- Escalates and resolves systemic control failures.
- Approves and reviews critical control performance standards.

**Layer 3 — Line Management (Line Managers / Supervisors)**
- Owns specific critical controls as named control owners.
- Conducts or oversees first and second-line verification activities.
- Takes immediate action when control failures are identified.
- Reports upward on control performance.

**Layer 4 — Operations (Frontline Workers, Maintenance, Contractors)**
- Performs pre-task critical control checks.
- Reports identified control failures immediately.
- Does not proceed when critical controls are absent or failed.

### 5.2 Critical Risk Identification

The process of identifying critical risks involves:

1. **Hazard identification workshops** using structured methods (HAZOP, What-If, hazard registers).
2. **Consequence-based filtering:** Asking "what is the worst credible consequence if this hazard is not controlled?"
3. **Credibility assessment:** Is the catastrophic scenario credible (not just theoretically possible)?
4. **Bowtie development** for shortlisted scenarios.
5. **Peer review and expert validation** of the identified critical risk list.
6. **Sign-off by executive and legal** to confirm the list is complete and accurate.

**The "Short List" principle:** A critical risk list should typically contain 10–20 risks. If an organisation identifies 50+ critical risks, it has almost certainly included non-critical risks in the list. The discipline is in the filtering — identifying the scenarios that can, without dedicated controls, produce catastrophic harm.

*Source:* ICMM (2015), section 2.1; CCPS, *Bow Ties in Risk Management* (2018).

### 5.3 Critical Control Identification

From each bowtie, critical controls are identified by applying the **criticality test:**

> *"If this control is removed or fails — with all other controls still in place — is the catastrophic outcome likely or significantly more likely?"*

If yes: it is a critical control.

**Categories of critical controls:**
- **Engineering controls:** Physical safeguards — guardrails, interlocks, pressure relief valves, structural supports.
- **Administrative controls:** Procedures, permits, exclusion zones, lock-out/tag-out (LOTO).
- **Human performance controls:** Competency requirements, verification behaviours, communication protocols.

**The hierarchy matters:** Engineering controls are inherently more reliable than administrative and human performance controls (Safe Work Australia hierarchy of controls). A system that relies primarily on administrative controls for critical risks is poorly designed and exposed.

### 5.4 Critical Control Performance Standards

Each critical control must have a documented performance standard. The performance standard answers these questions:

1. **What must the control do?** (Functional description)
2. **How well must it perform?** (Performance specification — measurable)
3. **What are the critical performance parameters?** (What would be monitored?)
4. **Who is responsible?** (Named control owner)
5. **How is it verified?** (Verification method — observation, test, document review)
6. **How often is it verified?** (Frequency — daily, weekly, monthly)
7. **What triggers escalation?** (TARP — trigger action response plan)
8. **What evidence confirms compliance?** (Records, documentation)

*Source:* ICMM (2015), section 3; UK HSE safety-critical element performance standard methodology.

### 5.5 Control Ownership

Each critical control must have a **named individual owner** — not a team, not a department, but a specific named person whose role includes accountability for that control's performance.

**Control owner responsibilities:**
- Understands the performance standard.
- Ensures verification activities are conducted.
- Reviews verification results.
- Escalates failures immediately.
- Manages corrective actions.
- Reports on control health at governance forums.

**Note on position vs person:** Ownership is attached to a **role or position**, not an individual name — so that ownership transfers automatically with role changes. When an individual changes position, the ownership transfers to their successor.

### 5.6 Verification Framework

**Verification levels:**

| Level | Who | What | When |
|-------|-----|------|------|
| L1 — Worker | Frontline worker, operator | Pre-task check: is the critical control in place before work begins? | Before every task |
| L2 — Supervisor | Supervisor / leading hand | Field verification: confirm critical controls are present and functioning during the task | Daily / each shift |
| L3 — Manager | Line manager | Field leadership interaction: observe and discuss critical control performance | Weekly / monthly |
| L4 — HSE/Assurance | OHS professional / internal auditor | Structured verification: sample critical controls across all areas | Monthly / quarterly |
| L5 — Independent | External or cross-functional | Independent audit of critical control system | Annual |

**Verification quality:** A critical distinction is between **activity verification** (did we do the check?) and **quality verification** (did the check actually test whether the control is effective?). Programs that track completion rates without assessing quality provide false assurance.

### 5.7 Trigger Action Response Plans (TARPs)

A **TARP** defines what must happen when a critical control fails or degrades. For each critical control, a TARP specifies:

- **Trigger condition:** The observable signal that the control is not meeting its performance standard.
- **Immediate action:** What the person who identifies the failure must do right now (typically: stop work, isolate, remove persons from the hazard zone).
- **Notification:** Who must be told, and how quickly.
- **Escalation:** If the control cannot be restored, who authorises a risk-managed alternative or work stoppage.
- **Restoration:** What must happen before normal work can resume.

TARPs must be pre-established and practised. Workers cannot make effective decisions about catastrophic risks under pressure without prior defined TARPs.

*Source:* ICMM (2015), section 4.

### 5.8 Assurance Framework and Lines of Defence

The assurance framework specifies how the organisation gains confidence that critical controls are effective across time and the full scope of operations. It has three lines:

**Line 1 (Operations):** Day-to-day pre-task checks, toolbox talks, supervisor observations. This is the primary source of verification — it happens every day.

**Line 2 (Management):** Management field visits, OHS inspections, critical control verification reviews. This provides an independent check on Line 1.

**Line 3 (Independent Assurance):** Internal audit, external audit, regulatory inspection, technical review by independent engineers. This tests whether the system as a whole is functioning.

**Key principle:** All three lines must operate independently. If Line 1 and Line 2 produce the same output (because Line 2 simply confirms what Line 1 reports), the assurance system is not generating independent confidence.

*Source:* IIA Three Lines Model (2020); ISO 19011:2018.

### 5.9 Critical Control Drift and Degradation

**Critical control drift** is the gradual, typically unnoticed, decline in control effectiveness over time. It occurs through:

- Physical degradation (equipment wear, corrosion, mechanical failure)
- Administrative drift (procedures no longer followed, permits not signed, training lapsed)
- Organisational drift (cultural normalisation of non-compliance, reduced resourcing)
- Change-induced drift (changes to work, plant, or organisation that inadvertently compromise controls)

**Signals of control drift:**
- Near-misses where a critical control "almost" failed.
- Verification findings showing degraded performance.
- Workarounds or shortcuts becoming common practice.
- Maintenance backlogs on safety-critical equipment.
- Training currency declining.

**Preventing drift:**
- Frequent, quality verification.
- Real-time reporting of deviations.
- Defined tolerances (how much degradation is acceptable before action?).
- Cultural signals from leadership that control integrity is non-negotiable.

## 6. Step-by-Step Implementation Roadmap

### 6.1 Prerequisites

Before beginning formal implementation, the organisation needs:

- **Executive sponsorship and mandate.** Critical control management will require resources, management time, and — critically — the willingness to stop work when controls are absent. This cannot be achieved without executive commitment.
- **Baseline risk intelligence.** An existing hazard register, incident history, regulatory obligations list, and understanding of the operations.
- **Designated project lead.** A senior safety professional or risk practitioner with authority to engage across operational areas.
- **Resource allocation.** Budget for facilitated bowtie workshops, software tools (if used), training, and ongoing verification activities.
- **Legal review.** Engagement with legal counsel to understand how critical risk identification interacts with legal duties and potentially privileged documents.

### 6.2 Phase 1 — Discovery and Gap Assessment (Weeks 1–6)

**Objectives:** Understand the current state, identify what exists, and determine the gap.

**Activities:**

1. **Review existing documentation:** Hazard register, risk register, incident records, previous audits, regulatory correspondence, previous safety assessments.
2. **Review existing controls:** What controls are currently documented? Are they maintained? Is there any existing verification?
3. **Stakeholder interviews:** Talk to operational leaders, supervisors, HSE staff, and workers about what they believe the biggest risks are and whether they have confidence in current controls.
4. **Regulatory review:** Identify all applicable legislation, codes of practice, and standards that govern critical risk management for this operation. In Queensland: WHS Act 2011, WHS Regulation 2011, relevant codes of practice, industry-specific regulations.
5. **Incident analysis:** Review the last 3–5 years of incidents and near-misses. Identify recurring themes and scenarios where critical controls were absent or failed.
6. **Gap assessment against ICMM CCM framework:** Use the ICMM CCM Implementation Guide as the benchmark. Score the organisation against each element.

**Outputs:**
- Current state assessment report
- Critical risk candidate list (preliminary)
- Gap analysis
- Recommendations for Phase 2

### 6.3 Phase 2 — Critical Risk Identification (Weeks 7–12)

**Objectives:** Produce an agreed, signed-off list of critical risks for the organisation.

**Activities:**

1. **Facilitated critical risk workshops:** Multi-disciplinary teams (operations, maintenance, engineering, HSE) work through a structured process to identify scenarios where catastrophic harm is credible.
2. **Apply the consequence filter:** For each scenario, ask: "If all controls fail, is the worst credible consequence death, multiple deaths, or catastrophic harm?"
3. **Credibility assessment:** Eliminate scenarios that are theoretically possible but not credibly harmful given operational context.
4. **Preliminary list validation:** Review list with technical experts, senior leaders, and operational personnel.
5. **Regulatory alignment:** Check the list against any regulatory expectations (e.g., MHF hazards, Queensland WHS Regulation requirements).
6. **Executive sign-off:** Present the final critical risk list to the executive/board for formal approval.

**Output:** Approved Critical Risk Register with 10–20 items.

### 6.4 Phase 3 — Bowtie Development (Weeks 13–20)

**Objectives:** Develop documented bowties for each critical risk, identifying all current controls.

**Activities:**

1. **Bowtie facilitation workshops** for each critical risk (typically half-day workshops per risk with relevant technical and operational staff).
2. **Identify threats** (what can initiate the unwanted event).
3. **Identify consequences** (what outcomes result from the unwanted event).
4. **Map current controls** to the bowtie — both preventive and mitigating.
5. **Identify escalation factors** — conditions that weaken barriers.
6. **Quality review** of each bowtie by a senior technical person not involved in the workshop.
7. **Validation** against historical incidents, near-misses, and technical knowledge.

**Output:** Documented bowties for all critical risks.

**Note:** Bowtie software (BowTiePro, ARM BowtieXP) is helpful but not required. Well-structured manual bowties are equally valid. The quality of the analysis is more important than the tool.

### 6.5 Phase 4 — Critical Control Identification (Weeks 18–24)

**Objectives:** From the bowties, identify which controls are critical (i.e., meet the criticality test).

**Activities:**

1. **Apply the criticality test** to each control on every bowtie: If this control is removed, would the catastrophic consequence become likely or significantly more likely?
2. **Validate with operational experts:** Do field operators and technical experts agree with the identification?
3. **Confirm the minimum set:** Identify the minimum number of critical controls that, if all present and effective, adequately control the critical risk.
4. **Document critical controls** in the Critical Control Register.

**Output:** Critical Control Register linking each critical control to its parent critical risk, with preliminary performance standard requirements.

### 6.6 Phase 5 — Critical Control Performance Standards (Weeks 22–30)

**Objectives:** Write performance standards for each critical control.

**Activities:**

1. **Performance standard workshops** for each critical control, involving the nominated control owner, technical experts, and operational personnel.
2. **Draft performance standard** using the standard template.
3. **Technical review** — engineer or subject matter expert confirms the performance specification is correct and achievable.
4. **Operational review** — operational leader confirms the standard is practical to implement and verify.
5. **Legal/regulatory review** — confirm the standard meets or exceeds regulatory requirements.
6. **Control owner sign-off** — the named control owner formally accepts the standard.
7. **Executive endorsement** for the complete set of performance standards.

**Output:** Documented performance standards for all critical controls.

### 6.7 Phase 6 — Ownership Assignment (Concurrent with Phase 5)

**Objectives:** Assign named control owners to every critical control.

**Key considerations:**
- The control owner must have the authority to act (direct control of the people, equipment, and budget required).
- The owner must understand the technical content of the performance standard.
- Ownership should be tied to the role, not the individual.
- The owner must be given time and resources to fulfil ownership duties.

**Output:** Ownership map in the Critical Control Register.

### 6.8 Phase 7 — Verification Design (Weeks 28–36)

**Objectives:** Design the verification system for each critical control.

**Activities:**

1. **Design verification activities** for each critical control — what will be observed, tested, or documented to confirm performance?
2. **Design verification checklists** — structured, unambiguous questions that a verifier can complete in the field.
3. **Define verification frequency** — based on risk (higher-risk controls need more frequent verification), control type (physical devices may need daily checks; system-level controls may need monthly).
4. **Assign verification responsibility** at each level (L1–L4).
5. **Design TARPs** for each critical control failure scenario.
6. **Design reporting mechanisms** — how verification results are captured, aggregated, and reported.

**Output:** Verification plans, checklists, TARPs, and reporting templates.

### 6.9 Phase 8 — Assurance Framework Design (Weeks 34–40)

**Objectives:** Design the independent assurance structure.

**Activities:**

1. **Design the annual assurance plan** — schedule of second and third-line verification activities across all critical controls and all operational areas.
2. **Design audit protocols** for critical control system audits.
3. **Design the governance forum structure** — who reviews what, how often.
4. **Design the executive dashboard** — what information reaches the board/executive, at what frequency, in what format.
5. **Design escalation protocols** — when does a control failure trigger escalation to the next governance level?

**Output:** Assurance framework document and annual assurance plan.

### 6.10 Phase 9 — Integration with Business Systems (Weeks 36–42)

**Objectives:** Ensure critical control management is embedded in operational business systems, not operating as a separate program.

**Key integration points:**
- **Incident investigation:** Every investigation must check whether critical controls were absent or failed.
- **Management of change (MOC):** Changes to plant, process, or organisation must be assessed for impact on critical controls before the change is implemented.
- **Procurement and contractor management:** Contractors performing work involving critical risks must demonstrate control performance to the same standards.
- **Maintenance management:** Work order systems must flag when maintenance is being performed on safety-critical equipment. Critical control performance standards must inform maintenance intervals.
- **HR and training:** Critical control competency requirements must be integrated into training plans and role descriptions.
- **Capital investment:** Proposals for new plant or significant modifications must address critical risk and critical control implications.

### 6.11 Phase 10 — Pilot (Weeks 40–50)

**Objectives:** Test the system in a defined operational area before full rollout.

**Pilot approach:**
- Select a representative operational area (ideally one with experienced leaders who are open to feedback).
- Implement all elements — verification routines, TARPs, reporting.
- Run the pilot for a full quarter.
- Review the pilot: What worked? What was impractical? What produced no useful information? What was missing?
- Incorporate learnings before full rollout.

### 6.12 Phase 11 — Full Rollout (Months 12–18)

**Objectives:** Implement across all operational areas.

**Key rollout considerations:**
- Stage the rollout by area — don't try to go everywhere simultaneously.
- Provide intensive leadership coaching at each site during initial implementation.
- Ensure local champions (typically supervisors or HSE professionals) are trained and supported.
- Establish regular progress reviews (fortnightly) during rollout.

### 6.13 Phase 12 — Continuous Improvement (Ongoing)

**Objectives:** Maintain, improve, and evolve the system.

**Annual review cycle:**
- Review the critical risk list — has the operational risk profile changed?
- Review all performance standards — are they still technically correct?
- Review the assurance plan — was it completed? What did it find?
- Review incident and near-miss data — what does it tell us about control performance?
- Review verification quality — are field checks genuine or perfunctory?
- Review control ownership — are owners active and effective?

### 6.14 Common Implementation Pitfalls

| Pitfall | Description | How to Avoid |
|---------|-------------|--------------|
| **Too many critical risks** | Listing 40+ items and calling them all critical | Apply the strict consequence/criticality filter; aim for 10–20 |
| **Too many critical controls** | Identifying every control as critical | Apply the criticality test rigorously; a control must pass the "remove it and catastrophe follows" test |
| **Paper compliance** | System is documented but not practised in the field | Make field verification visible and test it; don't accept paper-only verification |
| **Tick-box verification** | Verification becomes a routine sign-off without genuine assessment | Assess verification quality, not just completion; require narrative observations |
| **Ownership without authority** | Control owners are named but cannot actually influence the control | Assign owners who have line authority over the people and systems involved |
| **No TARPs** | System identifies failing controls but no one knows what to do | Design and practise TARPs before rollout; make them part of pre-task briefings |
| **Disconnected from operations** | CCM system runs parallel to operations without integration | Integrate into existing systems — MOC, maintenance, training, reporting |
| **Leadership disengagement** | Executives see CCM as an HSE program and delegate entirely | Keep executives actively engaged in governance; require quarterly board reporting |
| **Complexity creep** | System becomes so complex that it collapses under its own weight | Keep the system as simple as the risk requires; resist over-engineering |
| **No trigger for change** | System doesn't evolve when risk profile changes | Mandate annual review cycles; link review to incident learning and MOC |

## 7. Manager Education Guide

### 7.1 Executive Summary for Managers

**Why you need to read this:**

If your operation involves significant hazards — height, energy, confined spaces, mobile plant, pressure, hazardous materials, mechanical systems, structural loads, or public access — then somewhere in your area of responsibility is a combination of conditions that can kill people.

The question is not whether those conditions exist. They do. The question is whether the controls preventing those conditions from becoming fatalities are actually working — not just documented, but working.

Most safety management systems tell you whether people have signed off on procedures. Very few tell you whether those procedures are being followed or whether the physical safeguards they rely on are intact and functioning.

Critical control management fills that gap. It is the discipline of identifying your highest-consequence risks, specifying exactly what controls must be in place, and then systematically verifying that they are.

As a manager, you are not responsible for personally checking every control. You are responsible for ensuring that a system exists to check them, that the results come to you, and that when controls are failing, action happens.

### 7.2 Key Teaching Points for Managers

**Teaching Point 1: The difference between having a control and having an effective control.**

A procedure document is not a control. A training record is not a control. A risk assessment is not a control. A control is the physical or behavioural barrier that actually stands between your workers and catastrophic harm.

The management system documents the control. The verification confirms it is working.

*Question to ask yourself:* For each of your critical risks — do you have evidence that the controls are working today? Not last month. Not when the audit was done. Today.

**Teaching Point 2: Catastrophic risks are different from everyday risks.**

The rules that apply to everyday operational risk do not apply to catastrophic risk. For a minor injury risk, a "reasonable" control and periodic monitoring may be sufficient. For a risk that can kill multiple people, the control must be verified continuously, it must have a defined performance standard, and there must be a clear response when it fails.

The consequence changes the obligation. A 1-in-10,000 chance of a multiple fatality event requires more resources and more rigorous verification than a 1-in-10 chance of a lost-time injury.

*Teaching analogy:* An aircraft has redundant flight systems that are tested before every flight, not just annually. No airline accepts "the procedure says the system is maintained" as sufficient assurance before flight. Why should we accept less for our operations?

**Teaching Point 3: Control drift is invisible until it is catastrophic.**

Controls degrade slowly and silently. Equipment wears. Procedures get bypassed. Workarounds become normal. Workers adapt to imperfect systems and stop reporting. Supervisors stop checking because "we've never had a problem."

This is normalisation of deviance — and it preceded every major industrial disaster in the last 50 years.

Your job as a manager is to be the counter-force. Your job is to ask questions, visit the field, review verification data, and actively look for drift — not because you distrust your workers, but because the system pulls toward degradation over time.

**Teaching Point 4: The most important question is not "what happened?" but "what could happen?"**

Traditional safety management is reactive — it responds to incidents. Critical control management is proactive — it asks what could happen and ensures it won't.

The absence of incidents is not evidence that controls are working. It may simply mean that the confluence of failures has not yet aligned. When it does, the question "why didn't we see this coming?" will be asked about your management team.

**Teaching Point 5: Your role is governance, not inspection.**

You are not the inspector. You have inspectors, HSE staff, supervisors, and workers to perform field verification. Your role is to:
- Set expectations that critical controls will be maintained and verified.
- Review the evidence that verification is happening and controls are performing.
- Escalate when the evidence is missing or concerning.
- Hold people accountable when controls are allowed to degrade.
- Resource the system adequately.

### 7.3 Manager Talking Points

- "Before any work involving a critical risk begins, I want to know that the critical controls are in place."
- "When we find a critical control has failed, we stop work. We don't manage around it."
- "Show me the last verification result for [specific critical control]."
- "What is the TARP for this control failure?"
- "Who is the named owner of this critical control, and have they seen this result?"
- "How often is this control being verified, and by whom?"
- "What was the last time this control actually failed verification?"

### 7.4 What Good Control Ownership Looks Like

A good control owner:
- Can recite the performance standard for their control from memory.
- Reviews verification results weekly and follows up anomalies the same day.
- Walks the field at least monthly to see the control firsthand.
- Knows the history of their control — when it last failed, what was done, whether the fix held.
- Escalates immediately when the control degrades and it cannot be restored quickly.
- Treats a control failure as a serious event, not a paperwork issue.

A poor control owner:
- Did not know they were named as owner.
- Has not read the performance standard.
- Thinks verification is "someone else's job."
- Waits for the annual audit to find control problems.
- Approves "risk management" workarounds when the control is unavailable, without escalating.

### 7.5 Field Interaction — Questions Managers Should Ask

**Before work starts:**
- "What are the critical controls for this task?"
- "Have they been verified this shift?"
- "If that guardrail/interlock/isolation wasn't there, what would happen?"
- "What would you do if you found it was missing or not working?"

**During field visits:**
- "Can you show me the [specific control] and explain how you verify it?"
- "When was this last checked, and by whom?"
- "Has anything changed about this control recently?"
- "Have you ever found this control not working? What happened?"

**After an incident or near-miss:**
- "Which critical controls were relevant to this event?"
- "Were they in place?"
- "If they were in place, why didn't they prevent the outcome?"
- "If they were not in place, why not, and how long had they been absent?"

### 7.6 How to Identify Weak or Failing Controls

Warning signs that a critical control is failing or at risk:

- Verification records are incomplete, overdue, or show patterns of "pass" without any "fail" ever recorded.
- Workers describe the control as "more of a suggestion" or routine bypass.
- Maintenance backlog includes safety-critical equipment.
- Training currency for critical control activities is lapsing.
- Near-misses cluster around the same task or location repeatedly.
- Workers cannot articulate the critical control or what they would do if it failed.
- The performance standard hasn't been reviewed in over 12 months.
- The control owner has not visited the control in the field recently.

### 7.7 Decisions Based on Control Effectiveness, Not Paperwork

**The paperwork trap:** Many organisations approve work based on whether the risk assessment is signed, whether the permit is issued, and whether the procedure is current. None of these confirm that the critical controls are actually in place.

A signed permit for work at height does not confirm that the anchor points are rated correctly, that the harnesses have been inspected, or that the rescue plan has ever been rehearsed.

**The control effectiveness question:** Before approving high-risk work, ask not "is the paperwork complete?" but "are the critical controls in place and verified?"

This shift from bureaucratic approval to operational verification is the most important change a manager can make.

### 7.8 Manager FAQ

**Q: We have a safety management system — isn't this duplication?**

A: A safety management system tells you what you *should* do. Critical control management verifies that you are *actually doing it* for the risks that matter most. It is not duplication — it is the verification layer that most SMS frameworks omit.

**Q: How many critical risks should we have?**

A: Typically 10–20. If you have 50, you have almost certainly included things that are not truly critical. The discipline is in the filtering — not every risk is a critical risk.

**Q: Who does the verification?**

A: Everyone at their level. Workers do pre-task checks. Supervisors do shift observations. Managers do field visits. OHS staff do structured audits. External reviewers do independent assessments. It is a layered system.

**Q: What happens when a critical control is found to have failed?**

A: Work stops, the hazard zone is made safe, the control failure is investigated, corrective action is completed, the control is verified restored, and work resumes only with authorisation. The control owner and HSE are notified immediately. If the failure was systemic (not isolated), the system-level cause must be addressed.

**Q: What if we can't restore the control quickly?**

A: This is exactly what TARPs are for. The TARP defines what alternative risk controls can be implemented while the primary control is unavailable, and who can authorise continuation of work under the alternative. If no adequate alternative exists, work does not proceed.

**Q: Does this mean more bureaucracy?**

A: It means more operational discipline around the things that matter most. For 90% of work, nothing changes. For the 10% of work involving critical risks, the expectation is clear: verify the controls before you start. That is not bureaucracy — it is professionalism.

### 7.9 Myths vs Reality

| Myth | Reality |
|------|---------|
| "We've never had a fatality, so our controls are working." | Absence of an incident proves nothing. It may mean you haven't yet had the alignment of failures that causes catastrophe. |
| "We passed the audit, so we're compliant." | Audits check whether systems exist and are documented. They rarely verify real-time control performance. |
| "Our workers know what to do — training covers it." | Training is a component of some controls, but it is not a critical control by itself. Physical safeguards, procedures, and verification are separate requirements. |
| "Our risk matrix shows tolerable risk." | Risk matrices score risk assuming controls are working. If controls are degraded, the actual risk is higher than the matrix shows. |
| "Critical control management is an HSE program." | It is an operational management program. HSE facilitates. Managers own and operate it. |
| "We have too many risks to have only 15 critical ones." | You likely have many risks, but only a small number where the worst credible outcome is catastrophic and requires dedicated control verification. That is the correct filter. |
| "Verification takes too much time." | For the controls that stand between your workers and a fatality, insufficient time is not a defence. The question is whether you are allocating your management time to the things that matter most. |

### 7.10 Discussion Questions for Management Workshops

1. Can you name the top three critical risks in your area of responsibility? Can your supervisors? Can your frontline workers?
2. For one of your critical risks — pick one — what are the critical controls? Are they currently verified? By whom? When?
3. Have any critical controls in your area been found to have failed in the last 12 months? What happened? Was it reported? Was it corrected?
4. What would you do today if a supervisor called you and said a critical control was not in place and could not be restored immediately?
5. What is the signal that would tell you — right now, without visiting the field — that a critical control is degrading?
6. When did you last visit the field specifically to look at critical control performance — not general safety, but your critical controls specifically?
7. What production or time pressure, if any, currently exists that could be eroding critical control performance in your area?
8. Is your current risk register telling you anything useful about the real-time effectiveness of your critical controls?

## 8. Document Suite and Template Recommendations

### 8.1 Core Governance Documents

**Document 1: Critical Risk Management Framework**

- **Purpose:** Sets out the organisation's commitment, principles, scope, governance structure, and accountabilities for critical risk management.
- **Key sections:**
  - Policy commitment
  - Scope of application
  - Definitions
  - Critical risk identification process
  - Critical control identification and management process
  - Governance and accountability structure
  - Verification and assurance requirements
  - Reporting and escalation
  - Review and continuous improvement
  - Compliance with legislation and standards
- **Owner:** CEO / Executive
- **Review cycle:** Every 2–3 years or following significant change

**Document 2: Critical Control Management Standard**

- **Purpose:** Operational standard specifying how critical controls are identified, documented, owned, verified, and reported.
- **Key sections:**
  - Critical control identification methodology
  - Performance standard requirements
  - Ownership and accountability
  - Verification methods and frequencies
  - TARP requirements
  - Reporting requirements
  - Action tracking
  - Integration with other systems
- **Owner:** Senior HSE / Risk
- **Review cycle:** Annual

**Document 3: Bowtie Development Guide**

- **Purpose:** Instructional guide for developing and maintaining high-quality bowties.
- **Key sections:**
  - What a bowtie is and how it works
  - Scope and purpose in this organisation
  - Bowtie element definitions (threat, top event, consequence, barrier, escalation factor)
  - Facilitation methodology
  - Quality standards for bowtie elements
  - Software tool guidance (if applicable)
  - Review and validation process
  - Linking bowties to critical control register
- **Owner:** HSE Manager / Risk Manager
- **Review cycle:** As needed

### 8.2 Operational Documents

**Document 4: Critical Control Performance Standard Template**

- **Purpose:** Standardised template for documenting the performance standard for each critical control.
- **Key fields:**
  - Critical risk (parent MUE)
  - Critical control name
  - Control type (preventive / mitigating)
  - Control function (what it does)
  - Performance specification (measurable requirement)
  - Critical parameters (what must be monitored)
  - Control owner (name and position)
  - Verification method
  - Verification frequency by level (L1–L4)
  - Evidence of compliance
  - TARP conditions and responses
  - Escalation contacts
  - Last review date and reviewer
- **Owner:** Control owner (per document)

**Document 5: Critical Control Register**

- **Purpose:** Master register of all critical risks and their associated critical controls.
- **Key fields:**
  - Critical Risk ID
  - Critical Risk Name (MUE description)
  - Bowtie reference
  - Critical Control ID
  - Critical Control Name
  - Control type (Preventive/Mitigating)
  - Control owner (position and name)
  - Performance standard reference
  - Verification frequency
  - Current health status (Red/Amber/Green)
  - Last verification date
  - Overdue verifications
  - Open actions
- **Owner:** HSE Manager
- **Update frequency:** Real-time (ideally) / monthly minimum

**Document 6: Verification Plan and Checklists**

- **Purpose:** Documents what is verified, by whom, how often, and using what checklist.
- **Key sections:**
  - Verification schedule matrix (control × verifier level × frequency)
  - Field verification checklists for each critical control
  - Instructions for completing the checklist
  - What constitutes a pass / fail / observation
  - Reporting pathway
- **Owner:** Control owner (primary) / HSE (system-wide coordination)

**Document 7: Trigger Action Response Plans (TARPs)**

- **Purpose:** Pre-defined response protocols for each critical control failure scenario.
- **Key sections:**
  - Critical control identified
  - Trigger condition (measurable signal of control failure or degradation)
  - Immediate action (stop work / remove from hazard / isolate)
  - Notification requirements (who must be told immediately)
  - Escalation path
  - Alternative controls (if work must continue, what alternatives are authorised)
  - Restoration requirements (what must be done before normal work resumes)
  - Record-keeping requirements

### 8.3 Assurance Documents

**Document 8: Assurance Framework**

- **Purpose:** Defines the overall structure for gaining confidence that critical controls are effective.
- **Key sections:**
  - Three lines of assurance model
  - Responsibilities at each line
  - Annual assurance planning process
  - Scheduling and coverage requirements
  - Reporting and escalation
  - Integration with audit program
  - Competency requirements for assurance personnel

**Document 9: Annual Assurance Plan**

- **Purpose:** Scheduled calendar of all assurance activities for the year.
- **Key fields:**
  - Activity type (L2 field verification, L3 internal audit, L4 independent review, etc.)
  - Critical risks/controls in scope
  - Planned dates
  - Responsible party
  - Status
  - Completion and findings

**Document 10: Internal Audit Protocol — Critical Control System**

- **Purpose:** Structured protocol for auditing the critical control management system.
- **Key sections:**
  - Audit scope and objectives
  - Evidence requirements per element
  - Audit criteria (what "good" looks like)
  - Interview guide (questions for control owners, supervisors, workers)
  - Field observation requirements
  - Rating methodology
  - Reporting requirements
  - Action tracking

### 8.4 Incident and Learning Documents

**Document 11: Incident Investigation Prompts Linked to Critical Controls**

- **Purpose:** Ensures every investigation examines critical control performance.
- **Key questions:**
  - Which critical risks were relevant to this event?
  - Which critical controls should have prevented or mitigated the event?
  - Were those controls in place?
  - Were they performing to standard?
  - If not — how long had they been degraded?
  - Were there any prior verification findings that should have flagged this?
  - Did any TARP apply? Was it followed?

**Document 12: Management Review Template**

- **Purpose:** Structured template for management's quarterly and annual review of critical control system performance.
- **Key sections:**
  - Critical control health summary (RAG status)
  - Verification completion rates
  - Quality of verification findings
  - Overdue actions

## 11. Common Mistakes and Lessons Learned

### 11.1 Mistaking Documentation for Control Effectiveness

**The failure mode:** An organisation documents controls, develops procedures, and maintains records. Management receives reports that controls are "in place." No one verifies that the documented controls are actually performing.

**The consequence:** When failure occurs, the investigation finds that the documented controls had degraded months or years earlier. Workers had adapted. Supervisors had normalised the degradation. Management had no field-level visibility.

**Real-world exemplar:** The Pike River Mine explosion (2010, New Zealand) resulted in 29 deaths. The subsequent Royal Commission found that the mine had documented safety management system but that critical controls (methane monitoring, ventilation, ignition prevention) were not being verified or maintained. The system gave "the illusion of safety" (Royal Commission on the Pike River Coal Mine Tragedy, 2012, Vol. 1, p.11).

**Remedy:** Distinguish between system design (what the documents say) and system performance (what is happening in the field). Invest in field verification quality, not document quantity.

### 11.2 Confusing "Control Identified" with "Control Adequate"

**The failure mode:** The bowtie lists a control. Management assumes it is adequate. No one asks whether the control actually prevents the catastrophic outcome reliably, or whether it meets a testable performance standard.

**Consequence:** The control exists in name only. Its design has never been validated against the scenario it is supposed to address.

**Remedy:** Every critical control must have a performance standard that specifies measurable performance. Validation (confirming the design works) must precede verification (confirming it is working).

### 11.3 Superficial Verification ("Green Washing")

**The failure mode:** Verification becomes a routine administrative activity. Checklists are signed off without genuine field observation. "All controls in place" becomes the default answer because that is what managers want to hear.

**Consequence:** The organisation has verification records that show full compliance while the actual control state is unknown or poor.

**The "all greens, all the time" signal:** If a verification program never finds anything wrong, it is almost certainly not working. In any real operation, some controls will degrade some of the time. A verification program that never finds deviations is not finding the real world.

**Remedy:** Assess verification *quality*, not just completion. Require verifiers to document observations, not just tick boxes. Monitor the *rate of findings* — a zero-finding rate is a red flag, not a green one. Audit the auditors — test whether verification activities actually detect known control deficiencies.

*Source:* Hopkins, A., *Failure to Learn* (2008), Chapter 7.

### 11.4 Ownership Without Authority

**The failure mode:** A control owner is named, but they do not have the operational authority to direct resources, stop work, or require corrective action. They may be a technical advisor rather than a line manager.

**Consequence:** The control owner identifies failures but cannot compel action. Failures accumulate. The control degrades.

**Remedy:** Assign ownership only to people with genuine line authority over the people, equipment, and processes involved in the control. Technical advisors can support owners; they should not be owners unless they have the authority.

### 11.5 Over-Listing Critical Risks and Controls

**The failure mode:** Every significant risk is elevated to "critical" status. The critical control register balloons to 200+ controls across 40+ critical risks. The verification burden exceeds available management capacity. The system collapses.

**Consequence:** Organisations with over-listed critical risks typically abandon or perfunctorily complete verification activities. The volume signals seriousness but delivers nothing.

**Remedy:** Apply strict criteria. If the worst credible outcome is not catastrophic — not a multiple fatality, not irreversible mass harm — it is not a critical risk. Aim for 10–20 critical risks and a total of 50–150 critical controls across the full register.

### 11.6 Leadership Disengagement

**The failure mode:** Senior leaders approve the critical risk list and sign the framework, then delegate all further activity to the HSE team. They see critical control management as a safety program, not an operational leadership responsibility.

**Consequence:** The system produces reports that no one reads. Control failures are reported but no one acts with urgency. The program becomes a compliance exercise.

**Why it matters:** Hopkins (2008) identifies "executive blindness" — the tendency of executives to rely on filtered information rather than field reality — as a common precursor to major incidents. The Buncefield explosion (UK, 2005), the BP Deepwater Horizon disaster (2010), and numerous mine disasters share this pattern.

**Remedy:** Make executive and board engagement a non-negotiable system requirement. The executive should receive quarterly CCM dashboard reports and be required to ask penetrating questions. At least annually, the board should receive an independent assessment of critical control health. Senior leaders should make at least quarterly field visits specifically targeting critical control observation.

### 11.7 Treating MOC as Separate from CCM

**The failure mode:** The Management of Change process approves changes without assessing the impact on critical controls. A new piece of plant is introduced, a process changes, staffing is reduced — without checking whether critical controls are affected.

**Consequence:** Critical controls are inadvertently compromised by approved changes. The control that worked for the original design no longer functions for the modified design.

**Remedy:** Every significant change must trigger a critical control impact assessment as part of the MOC process. No change that affects a critical control should be approved without explicit review by the control owner and HSE.

### 11.8 Ignoring Contractor Control

**The failure mode:** Critical control requirements are enforced for direct employees but contractors are managed through different (typically weaker) safety systems. Contractors performing safety-critical work operate without demonstrating critical control compliance.

**Consequence:** Critical controls fail during contractor activities. Incident statistics for contractor fatalities consistently exceed those for direct employees in many high-risk industries.

*Source:* Safe Work Australia, *Work-related Traumatic Injury Fatalities* (annual series).

**Remedy:** Contractor scope of works must include explicit critical control requirements. Pre-mobilisation verification must confirm contractors understand and can meet critical control performance standards. On-site, contractor activities involving critical risks receive the same verification as direct-employee activities.

### 11.9 Neglecting Human Factors in Control Design

**The failure mode:** Critical controls are designed without considering whether humans can reliably perform them. A procedure that requires complex actions under time pressure, in poor lighting, with inadequate tools, while wearing cumbersome PPE — will not be performed reliably.

**Consequence:** The control fails not because people are careless but because the control was designed for ideal conditions that rarely exist.

**Remedy:** Apply human factors principles when designing human-dependent critical controls. Ask: "Under the actual conditions of work — time pressure, physical environment, cognitive load, fatigue, communication challenges — can a typical worker in this role reliably perform this control?" If not, redesign for reliability or add engineering controls to back up the human action.

*Source:* UK HSE, *Reducing Error and Influencing Behaviour (HSG48)* (2nd ed., 1999); Reason, J. (1997).

## 12. Practical Recommendations and Next Steps

### 12.1 Top 12 Practical Recommendations

**Recommendation 1: Start with executive sponsorship, not system design.**

No critical control management program succeeds without visible, sustained executive commitment. Before designing any system elements, secure formal executive sign-off on the program mandate, the resources, and the expectation that control failures will stop work.

**Recommendation 2: Keep the critical risk list short.**

A list of 10–15 critical risks with 4–6 critical controls each is far more powerful than a list of 50 risks with 200+ controls. Ruthlessly apply the catastrophic consequence filter. If the worst credible outcome is not catastrophic, it is not a critical risk.

**Recommendation 3: Write performance standards before launching verification.**

Do not begin asking people to verify things until you have documented, technically validated performance standards. Otherwise, verification is subjective and inconsistent — and you will not know whether a "pass" actually means the control is working.

**Recommendation 4: Name owners before naming systems.**

Before building dashboards and reporting tools, ensure every critical control has a named owner who understands their responsibility. Ownership is the operational engine of the system. Systems cannot compensate for absent ownership.

**Recommendation 5: Design TARPs before rollout.**

Workers and supervisors must know what to do when a critical control fails before they encounter that failure in the field. TARP design and training must precede operational rollout.

**Recommendation 6: Measure verification quality, not just completion.**

A verification completion rate of 95% is meaningless if the verifications are perfunctory. Build quality assessment into the system from the start. Require narrative observations. Audit verification quality periodically.

**Recommendation 7: Integrate with existing systems rather than running parallel.**

Build CCM into MOC, incident investigation, maintenance management, training, and contractor management. If CCM operates as a separate program, it will be the first thing dropped when pressure increases.

**Recommendation 8: Focus field visits on critical controls specifically.**

Manager field visits are valuable for many purposes, but for critical controls, they need to be targeted. Train managers to conduct field visits that specifically examine critical control performance — not just general safety walk-arounds.

**Recommendation 9: Use incidents and near-misses as learning inputs.**

Every significant incident and near-miss should be examined for critical control lessons. Was a critical control absent? Did it fail? Was there a TARP and was it followed? The answers to these questions drive system improvement more powerfully than any audit.

**Recommendation 10: Build the maturity assessment into the annual review cycle.**

Use the maturity model to benchmark the program at baseline and repeat annually. This provides evidence of improvement, identifies gaps, and maintains organisational focus.

**Recommendation 11: Do not let the program drift in its third year.**

The first two years of a CCM program typically show strong engagement because it is new. By year three, the novelty has worn off, the early easy improvements have been made, and complacency returns. Build deliberate renewal activities into the three-year program plan.

**Recommendation 12: Seek external benchmarking periodically.**

An external review by an independent expert — ideally someone with experience across multiple high-hazard organisations — provides a reality check on the organisation's self-assessment and surfaces blind spots that internal teams miss.

Available free from ICMM website. **Highly recommended as the primary operational reference.**

**2. Energy Institute (2013). *Guidance on the Use of Bowtie Diagrams in the Management of Major Accident Hazards.* Energy Institute, London.**

The authoritative guide to bowtie methodology. Covers bowtie elements, quality requirements, facilitation approach, and limitations. Essential for anyone developing or reviewing bowties. Available from the Energy Institute.

**3. Reason, J. (1997). *Managing the Risks of Organisational Accidents.* Ashgate, Aldershot.**

The foundational text on how organisational factors produce major accidents. Explains the Swiss cheese model, latent failures, and the management of safety barriers. Still the most influential text in the field. Essential background reading for all serious practitioners.

**4. Hopkins, A. (2008). *Failure to Learn: The BP Texas City Refinery Disaster.* CCH, Sydney.**

A masterclass in how major accidents happen even when organisations have documented safety management systems. Identifies the specific management and leadership failures that allowed critical controls to degrade. Essential reading for executives and HSE professionals.

**5. CCPS (2001). *Layer of Protection Analysis: Simplified Process Risk Assessment.* American Institute of Chemical Engineers, New York.**

The technical foundation of independent protection layers and the quantitative basis for understanding why critical controls must be independent and reliable. More technical than ICMM but essential for process safety contexts.

**6. ISO 31000:2018. *Risk Management — Guidelines.* International Organisation for Standardisation, Geneva.**

The international governance framework for risk management. Provides the overarching principles within which critical control management sits. Mandatory reference for enterprise risk integration.

**7. ISO 45001:2018. *Occupational Health and Safety Management Systems — Requirements with Guidance for Use.* International Organisation for Standardisation, Geneva.**

The international OHS management system standard. Clause 6.1.2 (hazard identification) and clause 8.1 (operational control) are most directly relevant. Provides the SMS shell into which CCM must be integrated.

**8. Safe Work Australia (2021). *Model Code of Practice: How to Manage Work Health and Safety Risks.* Safe Work Australia, Canberra.**

The Australian regulator's practical guidance on the risk management process under the WHS Act 2011. Covers the hierarchy of controls, reasonably practicable standard, and the risk management cycle. Free from Safe Work Australia website.

**9. UK Health and Safety Executive (2001). *Reducing Risks, Protecting People (R2P2).* HSE Books, Sudbury.**

The UK HSE's definitive statement on the ALARP principle. Explains how risk tolerability is assessed and how the grossly disproportionate test applies. Foundational for understanding the legal basis of critical control requirements.

**10. UK Health and Safety Executive (2013). *Managing for Health and Safety (HSG65).* 3rd ed. HSE Books, Sudbury.**

A practical management systems guide using the POPIMAR (Plan, Organise, Plan, Implement, Measure, Audit, Review) framework. Highly practical for senior managers needing to understand what a good SMS looks like.

### Secondary References

**11. Hollnagel, E. (2004). *Barriers and Accident Prevention.* Ashgate, Aldershot.**

Systematic treatment of barrier theory. Covers all types of barriers, their failure modes, and how to design barrier systems. Complements the Swiss cheese model with more nuanced analysis of barrier types and functions.

**12. Dekker, S. (2006). *The Field Guide to Understanding Human Error.* Ashgate, Aldershot.**

Essential for understanding how human factors interact with critical controls. Explains why blaming workers for control failures misses the systemic causes. Practical and accessible.

**13. Royal Commission on the Pike River Coal Mine Tragedy (2012). *Report.* New Zealand Government, Wellington.**

A comprehensive analysis of how a documented safety management system failed to prevent 29 deaths. Volume 2 in particular examines the specific barriers that failed. An essential case study in critical control failure.

**14. Hopkins, A. (2012). *Disastrous Decisions: The Human and Organisational Causes of the Gulf of Mexico Blowout.* CCH Australia, Sydney.**

Analysis of the Deepwater Horizon disaster with specific attention to why risk management systems failed to prevent catastrophe. Highly relevant to critical control design and governance.

**15. Vaughan, D. (1996). *The Challenger Launch Decision: Risky Technology, Culture, and Deviance at NASA.* University of Chicago Press.**

The canonical text on normalisation of deviance — the process by which organisations gradually accept conditions that should trigger alarm. Essential background for understanding why critical controls drift.

**16. CCPS (2007). *Guidelines for Risk Based Process Safety.* John Wiley & Sons, Hoboken.**

Comprehensive framework for process safety management across 20 elements. More detailed than ISO 45001 and specifically addresses catastrophic process risk. Essential reference for process and chemical industry applications.

**17. Safe Work Australia (2013). *Leading and Lagging Indicators: How to Use Them to Improve Work Health and Safety in Your Business.* Safe Work Australia, Canberra.**

Practical guidance on developing and using leading and lagging indicators for OHS. Free from Safe Work Australia.

**18. Mines Safety Taskforce (2010). *Improving Safety Outcomes for the Queensland Resources Sector.* Queensland Government, Brisbane.**

Queensland-specific analysis of mining safety and the drivers of fatal incidents. Relevant for Queensland operations.

**19. API RP 754:2016. *Process Safety Performance Indicators for the Refining and Petrochemical Industries.* American Petroleum Institute, Washington DC.**

The tier-based performance indicator framework for process safety. Provides the most widely adopted metrics framework for process safety performance. Relevant as a model for critical control indicator design.

**20. IIA (2020). *The Three Lines Model: An Update of the Three Lines of Defence.* Institute of Internal Auditors, Florida.**

Updated three-lines model for assurance and governance. The foundation of the assurance framework design for critical control management.

### Australian and Queensland Regulatory References

**21. Work Health and Safety Act 2011 (Qld) and (Cwlth).**
Primary duty of care legislation. Available free from Queensland legislation website and Federal Register of Legislation.

**22. Work Health and Safety Regulation 2011 (Qld).** 
Detailed regulatory requirements including major hazard facility provisions. Free from Queensland legislation website.

**23. Safe Work Australia. *Interpretive Guideline — Model Work Health and Safety Act: The Meaning of 'Reasonably Practicable'* (2013).**
Essential guide to interpreting the SFAIRP/reasonably practicable standard. Free from Safe Work Australia.

**24. Queensland Government, Office of Industrial Relations. Resources Safety and Health Queensland (RSHQ)** publishes extensive guidance for mining and resources operations in Queensland, including guidance on safety management systems and major hazard management. Free from the RSHQ website.

### 14.3 Manager Capability Development Pathway

**Stage 1 — Awareness (All Managers) — 4 hours**
- What critical risk management is and why it matters
- What critical risks and critical controls are
- Their role as leaders in the system
- Delivery: Structured workshop facilitated by senior HSE professional or external expert

**Stage 2 — Foundational Knowledge (All Managers) — 1 day**
- The organisation's critical risks and critical controls
- Reading and interpreting the Critical Control Register
- Understanding performance standards
- Control ownership responsibilities
- How to conduct a meaningful field visit for critical controls
- Delivery: Classroom / workshop with field practicum

**Stage 3 — Control Owner Competency (Control Owners) — Half day (additional)**
- Detailed requirements of their specific critical controls
- Performance standard interpretation
- Verification supervision
- TARP activation and management
- Monthly reporting requirements
- Delivery: Role-specific coaching session with HSE

**Stage 4 — Leadership Coaching (Senior Leaders) — Ongoing**
- Quarterly briefing on CCM system performance
- Annual field observation visit with expert debrief
- Annual executive review participation
- Delivery: Individual coaching, governance forums

**Stage 5 — Refresher (All Managers) — Annual, 2 hours**
- Review of any changes to critical risk list or performance standards
- Lessons from incidents involving critical controls
- Emerging issues and regulatory changes
- Delivery: Annual management briefing
